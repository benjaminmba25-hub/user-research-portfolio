# Project 1: Discovery Research - Universal Credit for Vulnerable Users

## Project Overview

**Research Question:** How might we improve the Universal Credit new claim experience for vulnerable users who need additional support?

**Context:** This discovery research project explores the needs of vulnerable customers making a new Universal Credit claim. It is informed by DWP's Advanced Customer Support (ACS) programme and the challenges identified in their 2024-2025 service transformation work.

**Approach:** GDS-aligned discovery research using qualitative methods to understand user needs, contexts, and pain points.

**Timeline:** 6-week discovery phase (simulated)

---

## Background Research

### Understanding the Context

Before conducting primary research, I reviewed publicly available information about DWP's services:

**DWP's Advanced Customer Support (ACS) Programme**
- ACS supports customers with additional needs across all DWP services
- The Visiting Service helps those who cannot access services digitally
- AI is being used to identify vulnerable customers from correspondence
- Key insight: 40% of Universal Credit claims require some form of additional support

**Key Challenges Identified from DWP Research:**
- Digital exclusion affects many vulnerable customers
- Customers often have to repeat their story multiple times
- Support needs are identified late in the process
- Limited flexibility in how customers can engage with services

**GDS Service Standard Requirements:**
- Understand user needs through research
- Design for the hardest to reach first
- Iterate based on user feedback
- Work in the open

---

## Research Objectives

1. Understand the end-to-end experience of vulnerable users making a UC claim
2. Identify pain points and barriers in the current journey
3. Explore users' mental models, expectations, and emotional states
4. Understand the context in which users interact with UC services
5. Identify opportunities for service improvement

## Research Methodology

### Methods Used

| Method | Purpose | Participants |
|--------|---------|--------------|
| **Semi-structured interviews** | Deep understanding of individual experiences | 8 participants |
| **Contextual inquiry** | Observe real-world behaviour and environment | 3 participants |
| **Diary study** | Capture experiences over time | 5 participants, 2 weeks |
| **Stakeholder interviews** | Understand organisational constraints | 4 DWP work coaches (simulated) |

### Participant Recruitment

**Target user groups (based on DWP's vulnerable customer profiles):**

1. **Users with mental health conditions** (anxiety, depression)
2. **Users with physical disabilities or mobility issues**
3. **Elderly users (65+) new to digital services**
4. **Users with limited English proficiency**
5. **Users experiencing homelessness or housing insecurity**
6. **Users with learning disabilities**

**Recruitment approach:**
- Partnered with Citizens Advice, Age UK, and local disability charities
- Used screening questionnaire to ensure diverse representation
- Offered £50 thank-you payment and travel expenses
- Provided options for in-person, phone, or video call participation

**Total participants:** 16 users + 4 stakeholders

### Ethical Considerations

- Informed consent obtained from all participants
- Data stored securely and anonymised
- Right to withdraw at any time
- Trauma-informed approach for sensitive topics
- Signposting to support services where needed

## Research Instruments

### Interview Discussion Guide (Excerpt)

```
DISCOVERY INTERVIEW: Universal Credit New Claim Experience

Introduction (5 mins)
- Thank participant, explain purpose
- Consent and data protection
- "There are no wrong answers—we're here to learn from your experience"

Section 1: Context & Background (10 mins)
- Tell me about your current situation
- What led you to apply for Universal Credit?
- Had you used DWP services before?

Section 2: The Claim Journey (20 mins)
- Walk me through what happened when you decided to apply
- How did you first try to make your claim?
- What was that experience like for you?
- [Probe for: emotions, barriers, help received, what was confusing]

Section 3: Support & Communication (15 mins)
- Did you need any help during the process?
- How did you find out about support available?
- How were you treated by DWP staff?
- What would have made it easier?

Section 4: Reflection & Improvement (10 mins)
- If you could change one thing about the process, what would it be?
- What advice would you give to someone in a similar situation?
- Is there anything else you'd like to share?

Closing (5 mins)
- Thank participant
- Confirm next steps
- Provide support information sheet
```

### Diary Study Prompts

Participants received daily prompts via WhatsApp or email:

**Day 1:** Tell us about your experience making your UC claim today. What happened? How did you feel?

**Day 3:** Did you need to contact DWP about anything? How did that go?

**Day 5:** Have you told anyone about your UC claim (family, friends, support worker)? What did they say?

**Day 7:** What's been the hardest part of this process so far?

**Day 10:** Looking back on the past two weeks, what's one thing that would have made this easier?

## Research Findings

### Key Insight 1: Digital Exclusion is Not Just About Access

**Finding:** Many participants had smartphones but lacked confidence using them for official purposes.

> *"I've got a phone, yeah. But I don't trust myself to do important stuff on it. What if I press the wrong thing?"* — Participant 3, 58, anxiety

**Implication:** Simply providing digital channels isn't enough. Users need confidence-building and clear error recovery.

---

### Key Insight 2: The Emotional Burden of Repeated Storytelling

**Finding:** Users with complex circumstances found it distressing to repeat their situation to multiple staff members.

> *"I had to tell five different people that I'd just come out of hospital. Each time it felt like reliving it."* — Participant 7, 42, recent hospital discharge

**Journey map showing the pain point:**

```
Claim Journey - Emotional Experience

[Apply online] ──────► [Phone call] ──────► [Jobcentre visit] ──────► [Follow-up call]
      │                      │                      │                      │
   Anxious               Frustrated            Distressed              Exhausted
   ("Am I doing          ("Long wait,          ("Had to tell my       ("Explained
   this right?")          explained again")      story again")          everything again")
```

**Implication:** A single view of customer needs and circumstances is essential for vulnerable users.

---

### Key Insight 3: Flexibility is About Control, Not Just Convenience

**Finding:** Users valued having choices about how and when to engage, but many weren't aware options existed.

> *"Nobody told me I could have someone with me. I went on my own and panicked."* — Participant 5, 34, autism

**What users wanted:**
- Choice of channel (online, phone, face-to-face)
- Choice of location (Jobcentre, community venue, home)
- Choice of time (outside standard hours for those with caring responsibilities)
- Choice of support (interpreter, advocate, family member present)

**Implication:** Services should proactively offer options, not wait for users to ask.

---

### Key Insight 4: Early Identification of Support Needs Changes Everything

**Finding:** Users whose vulnerability was identified early had significantly better experiences.

**Comparison:**

| User A: Late identification | User B: Early identification |
|------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Struggled with online form | Offered phone support from start |
| Multiple failed attempts | ACS Visiting Officer assigned |
| Reached crisis point | Received support before crisis |
| 6-week delay in first payment | Smooth process, on-time payment |

**Implication:** Proactive identification of support needs should happen at first contact, not after problems emerge.

---

### Key Insight 5: Trust is Built Through Small Interactions

**Finding:** Users' trust in the service was shaped by micro-interactions: tone of voice, being listened to, follow-through on promises.

> *"The lady I spoke to actually sounded like she cared. She remembered my name when I called back. That meant a lot."* — Participant 12, 67, recently bereaved

**Trust-building elements:**
- Warm, human tone (not scripted)
- Active listening and empathy
- Clear next steps and follow-through
- Consistency across touchpoints
- Being treated as an individual, not a case number

## User Personas

Based on the research, I developed three personas representing key user groups:

### Persona 1: "Margaret" - Recently Bereaved

**Demographics:** 72, widow, lives alone, first time managing finances

**Context:** Her husband always handled money matters. Now she's navigating UC for the first time while grieving.

**Goals:**
- Understand what she's entitled to
- Get support without feeling foolish
- Manage her money safely

**Frustrations:**
- Assumes she should know how to do this
- Worried about making mistakes
- Doesn't know who to ask for help

**Quote:** *"I feel like I'm bothering people when I ask for help."*

**Support needs:**
- Patient, non-judgmental guidance
- Clear written information to refer back to
- Option to bring a friend or family member

---

### Persona 2: "James" - Living with Mental Health Conditions

**Demographics:** 34, severe anxiety and depression, lives in temporary accommodation

**Context:** Lost job due to mental health crisis. No bank account, no ID, no permanent address.

**Goals:**
- Get financial support quickly
- Navigate the system without overwhelming anxiety
- Get help with practical barriers (ID, bank account)

**Frustrations:**
- Phone calls trigger panic attacks
- Forms ask for information he doesn't have
- Feels judged for his situation

**Quote:** *"I know I need help, but the thought of making a phone call makes me want to give up."*

**Support needs:**
- Non-phone contact options
- Support with practical barriers
- Single point of contact
- Trauma-informed approach

---

### Persona 3: "Aisha" - Limited English Proficiency

**Demographics:** 29, recently arrived in UK, two young children, speaks limited English

**Context:** Separated from husband, needs to support children. Struggling to understand UK benefits system.

**Goals:**
- Support her children
- Understand the system in her language
- Get help navigating unfamiliar processes

**Frustrations:**
- Information only in English
- Interpreters not always available
- Cultural differences in what's expected

**Quote:** *"I want to do the right thing, but I don't always understand what is being asked."*

**Support needs:**
- Information in multiple languages
- Interpreter services
- Culturally sensitive support
- Visual aids and simple language

├─ Touchpoints: GOV.UK website, phone line

├─ Touchpoints: Jobcentre, document upload, phone calls

├─ Emotions: Stressed, worried about being believed

├─ Touchpoints: Online account, phone calls
├─ User actions: Checks status, manages finances
├─ Emotions: Anxious, financial stress mounting
├─ Pain points: Unclear timelines, difficulty getting updates
└─ Opportunities: Proactive communication, clear timelines, advance payment option

├─ Touchpoints: Bank account, online account, Jobcentre
├─ User actions: Receives payment, reports changes
├─ Emotions: Relief (if successful), ongoing anxiety
├─ Pain points: Unexpected deductions, difficulty reporting changes
└─ Opportunities: Clear payment breakdown, easy change reporting, ongoing support

## Recommendations

### Priority 1: Early Vulnerability Identification (Must Have)

**Recommendation:** Implement AI-assisted screening at first contact to identify users who may need additional support.

**Rationale:** Research showed that early identification leads to significantly better outcomes.

**Implementation approach:**
- Train AI on patterns from previous ACS referrals
- Screen all initial contacts (online, phone, letter)
- Flag high-risk cases for immediate human review
- Trigger proactive outreach within 24 hours

**Success metrics:**
- % of vulnerable users identified within 48 hours of first contact
- Time to first support intervention
- User satisfaction scores (vulnerable users)

---

### Priority 2: Single View of Support Needs (Must Have)

**Recommendation:** Create a central record of user support needs that is accessible across all DWP services.

**Rationale:** Users are distressed by having to repeat their circumstances multiple times.

**Implementation approach:**
- Capture support needs at first contact
- Make visible to all relevant staff
- Allow users to view and update their needs
- Include accessibility requirements, communication preferences, support contacts

**Success metrics:**
- Reduction in "repeated storytelling" complaints
- Staff time saved per interaction
- User satisfaction with "being understood"

---

### Priority 3: Flexible Engagement Options (Should Have)

**Recommendation:** Offer genuine choice in how users engage with UC services.

**Rationale:** Users need control over how they interact, but many don't know options exist.

**Implementation approach:**
- Proactively offer options (not wait for users to ask)
- Phone, video, face-to-face at multiple locations
- Flexible appointment times (early morning, evening)
- Option to bring support person

**Success metrics:**
- Uptake of non-standard options
- Appointment attendance rates
- User satisfaction with flexibility

---

### Priority 4: Trauma-Informed Communication (Should Have)

**Recommendation:** Train all customer-facing staff in trauma-informed communication.

**Rationale:** Many users are experiencing trauma (bereavement, abuse, mental health crisis). Small interactions build or break trust.

**Implementation approach:**
- Mandatory training for all frontline staff
- Scripts that allow for human warmth
- Clear escalation pathways for distressed users
- Regular supervision and support for staff

**Success metrics:**
- User feedback on staff empathy
- Complaint rates
- Staff wellbeing scores

## Reflection

### What I Learned

This simulated discovery project deepened my understanding of:

- **The complexity of vulnerability** - It's not a single category but intersecting circumstances
- **The emotional dimension of services** - Users aren't just "completing tasks"; they're navigating life challenges
- **The importance of flexibility** - One size never fits all in government services
- **GDS principles in practice** - Starting with user needs sounds simple but requires genuine commitment

### Skills Demonstrated

This project showcases:

- **Research planning** - Designing a multi-method study with clear objectives
- **Qualitative methods** - Interviews, contextual inquiry, diary studies
- **Analysis & synthesis** - Thematic analysis, journey mapping, persona development
- **Stakeholder engagement** - Involving work coaches and service designers
- **Communication** - Clear, actionable findings and recommendations
- **Ethical practice** - Informed consent, trauma-informed approach
- **GDS alignment** - Following government user research standards
